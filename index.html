National Parks see record visitation despite pandemic

By Gillian Smith

Semele Turro-Yared was forced to cancel her year-long trip around the world – which was supposed to begin in April 2020 – when COVID-19 hit. Instead, she and her husband Chris put their international travel plans on hold, purchased a van, and spent the past year traveling around the United States.

Semele and Chris are only two of thousands of people who decided to visit parks and monuments in the National Park Service during the pandemic. At Cuyahoga Valley National Park – located between the Ohio cities of Cleveland and Akron – more than 517,000 more people visited the park during 2020 than did in 2019 – a 23% increase in visitation, according to data from the park service.

For many, the National Parks were the only places that offered respite from the quarantine and isolation imposed upon the country in the hopes of stopping the spread of the coronavirus.

“We could have stayed doing the vanlife forever,” Turro-Yared said. “From day one, as we’re falling asleep in the van right on a lake, we just said to each other, ‘This is amazing.’”

During the height of the coronavirus pandemic, Americans were put under varying degrees of restrictions – statewide mask mandates, limited grocery store hours, remote school and work – that left many people feeling trapped inside their homes. For those who had access to transportation, the parks became a place of refuge, where they could be outside and enjoy nature while keeping those around them safe.

Of the top 10 National Parks, seven saw significantly more visitors during September 2020 than they had in September 2019, including Yellowstone, which had 143,996 more visitors than the same time the previous year. While the Grand Canyon saw nearly 330,000 fewer visitors in September 2020, the Great Smoky Mountains welcomed 126,845 more visitors than the year before.
